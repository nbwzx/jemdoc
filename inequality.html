An inequality

Problem

If \(x_{1}, x_{2} \geq 0,\left\{\begin{array}{l}m \geq n \geq 0 \\ s \geq t \geq 0 \\ m+n \geq s+t \\ n \geq t\end{array}\right.\), then \( \displaystyle\left(\frac{x_{1}^{m}+x_{2}^{m}}{x_{1}^{n}+x_{2}^{n}}\right)^{\frac{1}{m-n}} \geq\left(\frac{x_{1}^{s}+x_{2}^{s}}{x_{1}^{t}+x_{2}^{t}}\right)^{\frac{1}{s-t}} \)

Proof

After taking the logarithm of both sides, the original inequality becomes \[ \frac{\ln \left(x_{1}^{m}+x_{2}^{m}\right)-\ln \left(x_{1}^{n}+x_{2}^{n}\right)}{m-n} \geq \frac{\ln \left(x_{1}^{s}+x_{2}^{s}\right)-\ln \left(x_{1}^{t}+x_{2}^{t}\right)}{s-t} \] First, let us calculate the 1,2,3 derivatives of \(\varphi(r)=\ln \left(x_{1}^{r}+x_{2}^{r}\right)\)

Setting \(\alpha=x_{1}^{r}+x_{2}^{r}, \beta=x_{1}^{r} \ln x_{1}+x_{2}^{r} \ln x_{2}, \gamma=x_{1}^{r}\left(\ln x_{1}\right)^{2}+x_{2}^{r}\left(\ln x_{2}\right)^{2}, \theta=x_{1}^{r}\left(\ln x_{1}\right)^{3}+x_{2}^{r}\left(\ln x_{2}\right)^{3}\)

we have \(\displaystyle \varphi^{\prime}(r)=\frac{\beta}{\alpha}\), \(\displaystyle \varphi^{\prime \prime}(r)=\frac{\alpha \gamma-\beta^{2}}{\alpha^{2}}\), \(\displaystyle \varphi^{\prime \prime \prime}(r)=\frac{\alpha^{2}(\alpha \theta-\beta \gamma)-\left(\alpha \gamma-\beta^{2}\right) 2 \alpha \beta}{\alpha^{4}}=\frac{\alpha^{2} \theta-3 \alpha \beta \gamma+2 \beta^{3}}{\alpha^{3}}\)

Setting \(y_{1}=x_{1}^{r}, y_{2}=x_{2}^{r}, z_{1}=\ln x_{1}, z_{2}=\ln x_{2}\)

then \(\alpha^{2} \theta-3 \alpha \beta \gamma+2 \beta^{3}\)

\(=\left(y_{1}+y_{2}\right)^{2}\left(y_{1} z_{1}^{3}+y_{2} z_{2}^{3}\right)-3\left(y_{1}+y_{2}\right)\left(y_{1} z_{1}+y_{2} z_{2}\right)\left(y_{1} z_{1}^{2}+y_{2} z_{2}^{2}\right)+2\left(y_{1} z_{1}+y_{2} z_{2}\right)^{3}\)

\(=y_{1} y_{2}\left(y_{2}-y_{1}\right)\left(z_{1}-z_{2}\right)^{3}\)

since \(y_{1} \leq y_{2} \Longleftrightarrow x_{1} \leq x_{2} \Leftrightarrow z_{1} \leq z_{2}\)

\(y_{1} \geq y_{2} \Longleftrightarrow x_{1} \geq x_{2} \Leftrightarrow z_{1} \geq z_{2}\)

so \(\left(y_{2}-y_{1}\right)\) \(\left(z_{1}-z_{2}\right)^{3} < 0 \)

so \(\alpha^{2} \theta-3 \alpha \beta \gamma+2 \beta^{3} < 0\)

we have \(\varphi^{\prime \prime \prime}(r) \leq 0\)

Returning to our original problem.

By Hölder's inequality, we have \(\displaystyle\left(x_{1}^{r}+x_{2}^{r}\right)^{\frac{s-t}{r-t}}\left(x_{1}^{t}+x_{2}^{t}\right)^{\frac{r-s}{r-t}} \geq x_{1}^{s}+x_{2}^{s}\)    (here \(\left.r \geq s \geq t\right)\)

we have \(\displaystyle\left(\frac{x_{1}^{r}+x_{2}^{r}}{x_{1}^{t}+x_{2}^{t}}\right)^{\frac{1}{r-t}} \geq\left(\frac{x_{1}^{s}+x_{2}^{s}}{x_{1}^{t}+x_{2}^{t}}\right)^{\frac{1}{s-t}}\)

Setting \(r=m+n-t\), which satisfies \(r \geq s \geq t\)

Thus, it suffices to prove the inequality for \(m+n=s+t\)

Since \(m \leq s\)

setting \(\displaystyle f(q)=\frac{\ln \left(x_{1}^{q}+x_{2}^{q}\right)-\ln \left(x_{1}^{p-q}+x_{2}^{p-q}\right)}{q-(p-q)} \quad \left(\frac{p}{2} \leq q \leq p\right)\)

\(\displaystyle f^{\prime}(q)=\frac{\left(\varphi^{\prime}(q)+\varphi^{\prime}(p-q)\right)(2 q-p)-2(\varphi(q)-\varphi(p-q))}{(2 q-p)^{2}}\)    (here and later \(\varphi^{\prime}(p-q)\) means \(\left.\varphi^{\prime}(r)\right|_{r=p-q}\) )

Setting \(g(q)=\left(\varphi^{\prime}(q)+\varphi^{\prime}(p-q)\right)(2 q-p)-2(\varphi(q)-\varphi(p-q))\)

then \(g^{\prime}(q)=\left(\varphi^{\prime \prime}(q)-\varphi^{\prime \prime}(p-q)\right)(2 q-p)\)

Setting \(h(q)=\varphi^{\prime \prime}(q)-\varphi^{\prime \prime}(p-q)\)

then \(h^{\prime}(q)=\varphi^{\prime \prime \prime}(q)+\varphi^{\prime \prime \prime}(p-q) \leq 0\)

So \(h(q) \leq h\left(\frac{p}{2}\right)=0\)

and \(g(q) \leq g\left(\frac{p}{2}\right)=0\)

we get \(f^{\prime}(q) \leq 0\), which means \(f(q)\) is decreasing when \(\frac{p}{2} \leq q \leq p\).

Setting \(p=m+n\)

since \(m \leq s, \frac{p}{2} \leq s \leq p, \frac{p}{2} \leq m \leq p\)

we have \(f(m) \geq f(s)\)

which gives us \(\displaystyle\frac{\ln \left(x_{1}^{m}+x_{2}^{m}\right)-\ln \left(x_{1}^{n}+x_{2}^{n}\right)}{m-n} \geq \frac{\ln \left(x_{1}^{s}+x_{2}^{s}\right)-\ln \left(x_{1}^{t}+x_{2}^{t}\right)}{s-t}\)

Q. E. D.

Generation

Actually, this inequality cannot be generalized to the case of three or more variables.

The simplest counterexample is as follows:

\(x_1=2, x_2=2, x_3=3, m=2, n=1, s=3, t=0\)

In this case \(\operatorname{LHS}=\frac{17}{7}\) and \(\operatorname{RHS}=(\frac{43}{3})^{\frac{1}{3}}\)

so \(\operatorname{LHS} < \operatorname{RHS}\)


Additional, it seems that the inequality \[\left(\frac{n-1+t^p}{n-1+t}\right)^{\frac{1}{p-1}} < \left(\frac{n-1+t^{p+1}}{n}\right)^{\frac{1}{p+1}}\]

holds if and only if \( 1 < t < (n-1)^{\frac{2}{p+1}}\)    (here \( t>0 \) and \(p>1\))

Background

I saw such an inequality in high school \( \displaystyle \frac{x^{2}+y^{2}}{x+y} \geq \sqrt[3]{\frac{x^{3}+y^{3 }}{2}} \quad(x, y>0)\)

It was very difficult to prove, and I proved it by brute force approach.

Then, I wondered why this 2-variable inequality is so tight, and whether it can be generalized.

And I proposed a conjecture in the general case and proved it.

Finally, I rewrited my proof in \(\LaTeX\) when I was studying at the university.
